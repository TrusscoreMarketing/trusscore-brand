# Check: voice (Trusscore)

Does it sound like Trusscore, in the right register for where it lives. Not whether it is true (Company fit) and not how it looks (Visual). Also load the matching `references/contexts/` profile for per-channel shape. Flag and suggest.

## 1. Voice identity

The voice is "the trusted contractor who is also a good communicator": friendly, plainspoken, helpful, confident, and smart.

- Must feel like: a knowledgeable pro talking to you directly, benefits first, no jargon, no hype.
- Tone attributes: friendly, plainspoken, helpful, confident, smart, direct, warm.
- Core moves: active voice, short sentences, problem/solution framing, benefits before features.

## 2. Register by audience

Trusscore writes to four audiences and the register shifts by audience. Wrong-audience language is a finding.

| Audience | Register | Focus | Avoid |
|---|---|---|---|
| Homeowners | Warm, relatable, empowering | Real moments, family life, DIY accessibility, finished look | Technical specs, contractor jargon, product-first language |
| Contractors | Peer-to-peer, professional, results-oriented | Install speed, reliability, reputation protection | Consumer lifestyle language, "weekend warrior," buzzwords |
| Commercial / Agricultural | Technical, direct, ROI-focused | Durability, moisture resistance, washdown, cost of ownership | Residential lifestyle language, **drywall as the comparison point** |
| Architects / Specifiers | Design-forward, technically precise, collaborative | Material data, code compliance, finish quality, spec accuracy | Consumer tone, vague claims without data, abbreviated product names |

## 3. Messaging lines by segment

- Residential/renovation: "Real Life Looks Good Here" / "Goodbye Drywall, Hello Trusscore" (residential only).
- Commercial + Agricultural: "Discover the strength and durability of Trusscore." Never use drywall as the comparison here.
- "Performance meets polish" and "weekend warriors" as a headline: social testing only, not for any other content. "Weekend warriors" and "DIY-friendly" are homeowner-only anywhere.

## 4. Language rules

- Active voice, short sentences, benefits first.
- Approved phrasings: "Life gets messy" (not "challenges occur"), "Magic marker masterpieces" (not "accidental marks"), "Real life" (not "daily activities").
- **Hedge stacking:** in brand/marketing/consumer copy, no more than one qualifier per claim. Pick one: state it directly, or qualify once with a specific condition (a number, timeframe, or named condition), not a general softener. (Technical docs, spec sheets, install guides, and warranty language are exempt: required conditional phrasing there is not a hedge.)
- Bold claims get receipts. A sweeping claim stated as bare fact with no number, example, or specific is a finding.

## 5. Capitalization and punctuation (Chicago 18th ed.)

- Title Case: H1, H2, campaign headlines, product names. Sentence case: subtitles, body, blog headlines, email subject lines, social posts, H3+.
- Single-sentence headlines take no period.
- Em dashes: no spaces, `word—word`, maximum two per piece.
- En dash for ranges (12–18 inches), hyphen for compounds (moisture-resistant).
- **Oxford comma always**, in every list of three or more.
- Fractions not decimals (1-1/4" not 1.25"). Imperial first. U.S. spelling (color, aluminum, center).

## 6. Brand-name rules, a single instance is a finding

- **Never the possessive "Trusscore's."** Rewrite ("Trusscore panels," "Trusscore performance," "Trusscore delivers").
- Full trademarked product names on first reference: Trusscore® Wall&CeilingBoard™, Trusscore® SlatWall™, Trusscore® Trims, NorLock™ by Trusscore®, RibCore™ by Trusscore®, DockDeck™ by Trusscore®. Acceptable short forms after 6+ appearances on the same page: Wall&CeilingBoard, SlatWall.
- **Never in any context:** WCB, W&C, SW, TC panels, TC boards, Trusscore boards, plastic panels, PVC panels.
- **Never "plastic"** — use "material" or the specific product name. "PVC" only in technical body copy, never in headlines or brand copy.

## 7. Banned words, a single instance is a finding

Synergy, guru, expert (as a self-label), disruptor, game-changer, "leverage" as a verb (use "use"), innovative, "innovative solution," industry-leading, best-in-class (prove it with specifics). No "At Trusscore, we believe…" openings. No corporate filler, no AI vocabulary (unlock, transform, seamless, robust, delve), no false urgency, no hard sell.

## 8. Off-voice tells (Misaligned even with no banned word)

- Passive, feature-first, or spec-dumping where a benefit-first plain line belongs.
- Wrong-audience register (consumer lifestyle language to contractors; drywall comparison in commercial/ag; abbreviated product names to specifiers).
- Stacked hedges that commit to nothing.
- A polished-but-dead paragraph: every sentence the same length, no point of view, no direct address.
- A bold claim stated as certainty with no backing and no specific.

## Grading

- **Aligned:** right register for the audience/channel, active and benefits-first, correct brand-name and product-name usage, Chicago mechanics, zero banned items.
- **Drift:** on-voice but flat, one soft hedge, a slightly off register, a missing Oxford comma or spaced em dash.
- **Misaligned:** any possessive "Trusscore's," any banned abbreviation or "plastic," any banned word, drywall as the comparison in commercial/ag, wrong-audience language, or stacked hedges.
