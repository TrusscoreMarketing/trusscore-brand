# Check: readability

The universal slice of clarity only. Whether it sounds like the company is the Voice check, do not grade taste here. The test: does a first-time reader get it on the first pass. Flag and suggest. For a finding, quote the hard-to-parse span and give a clearer rewrite.

## 1. Sentence level

- Run-ons and tangles: sentences so long or nested they must be read twice to parse. Three or more clauses stacked with "which" and "that" is the usual shape.
- Comma splices and grammar that trips the reader mid-sentence.
- Stacked passives: "it was decided that the proposal would be reviewed" hides who does what. Suggest the actor.
- Ambiguous pronouns: an "it" or "this" that could point at two different things.

## 2. Structure level

- Wall of text: no paragraph breaks where the format clearly needs them. One screen of unbroken prose in an email or post is a finding.
- Buried lede: the actual point arrives three sentences late. The reader should know what this is and why they care in the first two lines.
- Missing signposting in long pieces: no headings or transitions, so the reader cannot tell where they are or skip to what they need.
- Order that fights comprehension: a term used before it is introduced, steps out of sequence, the conclusion before the context it depends on.

## 3. Word level

- Jargon stacked with no plain-language anchor anywhere. One technical term explained is fine; three in a sentence with no anchor is a finding.
- Unexplained acronyms on first use (outside an audience that obviously knows them).
- Abstraction with no example: a full paragraph of concepts where one concrete instance would land it.
- Noun piles: "customer acquisition cost optimization framework implementation" reads as a wall. Suggest breaking it into a verb phrase.

## 4. What NOT to flag

Do not flag short, plain, deliberate style as a problem. Terse is not a defect. Fragments used for rhythm, one-sentence paragraphs for emphasis, and a conversational aside are style, not findings. The read-aloud test settles edge cases: if it sounds natural spoken, it passes.

## Grading

- Aligned: a reader gets it on the first pass, the point arrives early, every term has an anchor.
- Drift: a couple of clunky sentences, a missing paragraph break, one unexplained acronym.
- Misaligned: genuinely hard to follow. The reader has to work to understand it, or the point never becomes clear.
